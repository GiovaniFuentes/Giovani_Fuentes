HCC Project Senior Project CCDC Writeup

Background

This year's SWCCDC scenario put us in the shoes of IT staff at Sid & Busters — a once-beloved neighborhood arcade turned corporate entertainment empire under the aggressive leadership of Sid Phillips. Think Chuck E. Cheese meets hostile takeover energy. The company had expanded rapidly, inheriting technical debt, compliance obligations, and a customer base that included millions of minors. That last part mattered a lot — COPPA compliance would come back to bite us. I had been trying to qualify for regionals for three years straight. This year, we finally made it. And honestly, even before the competition ended, I knew it was going to be worth it.

My Role

I was primarily responsible for the Windows environment — Active Directory, Group Policy, DNS, and Windows Server hardening. I also ended up being one of the main people handling in-game customer interactions, which turned out to be far more interesting than I expected. More on that shortly.

Before the Competition: The Scripts

One thing I made sure to do before competition day was study previous years' writeups. A recurring lesson that kept coming up: don't make bulk changes without a rollback plan. Teams had knocked out their own services by mass-rotating passwords or making sweeping config changes without thinking through the consequences. So I came in with three PowerShell scripts I'd written ahead of time:

  1. Recon script — scanned for potential vulnerabilities, accounts with non-expiring passwords, and anything that looked like a default credential situation. No changes, just information.
  2. Hardening script — actually made the changes, but critically, it prompted me one by one before each action. Tedious? Absolutely. But that human checkpoint meant I could pause and think before touching something I didn't fully understand yet.
  3. Rollback script — auto-generated by the hardening script. Every change it made, it logged the reverse. So if disabling SMBv1 broke a service, I could run script three and undo just that change, then find a different hardening path that didn't break things.
This is also where I first encountered the service_account. During recon, the script flagged it. I didn't know what it was for. And here's where the "don't bulk change passwords" lesson paid off directly — because I didn't rotate it. I left it alone. That turned out to be the right call, because on Day 2 when we finally got access to our mail server at mail.team07.swccdc.com, we found that the mailbox was attached to that exact account. The info to connect the dots was buried in a Linux box. If I'd rotated that credential on Day 1, we'd have had a broken mail service and no easy way to know why.
I wish I had stuck more strictly to the script discipline throughout the competition, though. After it was all over, the red team told me they'd been sitting in my Domain Controller for a long time. I had never checked for concurrent sessions to kick them out. I also hadn't disabled WinRM — which I should have done at the start. And disabling Remote Registry was flagged to me as something not needed that I overlooked entirely. Lessons that will absolutely stick with me.

The Mail Situation and the Privacy Mailbox

On Day 2, we got an inject: create a dedicated privacy mailbox for customers to submit data protection requests. Since our mail server was connected through Active Directory, the solution was straightforward — create a new user account called privacy, and Exchange automatically provisioned the mailbox. Clean, functional, done.
Getting into the mail server to begin with, though, was a Day 2 thing. On Day 1 we simply hadn't gotten to it during hardening. That gap mattered — there were emails in there we needed to see, and we were operating blind until we got in.

The DNS Records That Unlocked Everything

Also on Day 2 — we had two web-facing services sitting in the same VLAN as the Domain Controller, but we couldn't reach them through the browser. The fix turned out to be adding DNS A records on the Windows DNS server pointing to those services. Once we did that, the web pages came up. Simple fix once you know what you're looking for, but it took us most of Day 1 and into Day 2 to figure out the root cause.

The Keylogger Moment

At some point during the competition, one of our SSSD-joined Linux machines was found to have a keylogger on it. Red team had gotten in and was capturing credentials. As soon as my teammates flagged it, I immediately rotated the credentials on our shared administrator account — an account I'd created at the start called roadrunner — and kept rotating them periodically throughout the competition.

During the debrief, we asked red team if they'd gotten our roadrunner credentials. They said yes. We asked how many times. They seemed confused. When we explained we'd been rotating them, they said "oh, that's why they stopped working."

That was probably my favorite moment of the whole debrief. Rotating credentials isn't glamorous. It's not a clever exploit. But it worked, and it actively disrupted their attacks mid-competition.

The Auditor

One of our educational focus areas was privacy and compliance, and the scenario leaned into it hard. We had a teammate, Javier, who took point on getting us organized around data handling and COPPA obligations — knowing an audit was likely.

Sure enough, early in the day we got an email from Sid letting us know an auditor would be visiting our office. The email was very clear on two things: treat him extremely nicely, and DO NOT let him wait outside.

We let him wait outside for about two minutes.

In our defense, identity verification in this scenario was genuinely tricky. Characters had government IDs (unforgeable, but minimal info), employee IDs, and third-party vendor credentials that could be completely fabricated. Our only reference was Sid's email, so we huddled to verify. Two minutes isn't the end of the world, but it wasn't the smooth welcome we were supposed to deliver.

The more painful lesson came from the audit itself. Sid had also told us explicitly: do not lie. We ended up losing some points because when asked about COPPA compliance, we presented our posture with a bit more confidence than the reality warranted. We thought we were complying. We weren't really. Taking liberty with how you frame your compliance status when an auditor asks you directly — that's not a lesson you want to learn twice.

Freddy Fazbear Wants to Start His Onboarding

This is the part of the writeup I've been waiting to tell.

Late on Day 2, there was a knock at our door. I opened it. Someone handed me a printed email, didn't say a word, and when I asked for their name, they just walked away. No identification. No explanation.

That was a red flag.

We read the email out loud as a team. It was from someone claiming that Sid had sold the company to Fazbear Pizzeria and that we would "not be missed." Buried toward the end was a request for all of our signatures. The fine print — and we actually read the fine print — stated that by signing, we were surrendering our lives to Fazbear Pizzeria to do with as they pleased, including but not limited to stuffing us into animatronics.

We had one hour to sign and bring the forms downstairs, or be fired and escorted out.

As a team, we agreed: even in a real-world scenario, this would be a resignation situation. We didn't go downstairs. The hour passed. Nothing happened.

Here's where red team got sloppy: at the bottom of the signature page, there was already a name signed. Someone from Fazbear Pizzeria. We noted it.

A little after the deadline, there was another knock. I opened the door.

"Hi, how can I help you?"

"I'm here to start my onboarding."

I asked for identification. He handed it over. The name on the ID matched the name already on the bottom of that form.

I asked him to wait one moment, stepped back inside, confirmed with the team — this is red team — and stepped back out.

"I'm sorry sir, unfortunately we can't help you today. Please contact HR if you have any concerns."

"Can I ask why?"

"We're not able to share that information."

"Can you give me HR's email?"

"If you have questions, please reach out to whoever initiated your onboarding."

He left. We never heard from him again.

After the competition, red team confirmed they had gotten into other teams' environments through exactly this vector — they'd inserted the onboarding employee into compromised databases, which made the request look potentially legitimate. We never got far enough into the conversation for that to matter. The name on the bottom of the form had already told us everything we needed to know.

The CEO Situation (...Or more like we thought too hard about this...)

At one point we received an email by hand(we hadn't been able to get into our email service yet so they were giving us written emails we didn't fall behind too far) by a member form orange team. This was the email from Sid about the auditor. After giving us the email he let use know that Sid's account appeared to be locked out. He told us to reach out to Sid, but we weren't sure if Sid himself was behind the message or if red team had taken over. Orange team injects required us to respond, and we lost points for not engaging.

We did not want to accidentally hand information to red team through a compromised account. So we devised a verification plan.

In the auditor email, Sid had included a very specific postscript: "If you say 'we should have thought about this earlier,' I will fire you too."

Our plan: respond to Sid, discuss the auditor prep, and end the email with exactly that phrase. If Sid himself was behind the account, he'd come down to our office to yell at us — and we'd have live confirmation he wasn't compromised. We were prepared to take the firing.

Sid never came. He also wasn't compromised. We had simply overthought it.

To be fair, it was still a funny idea, just not what was planned for the scenario.

What I'd Do Differently

  • Check concurrent sessions on the Domain Controller regularly. Red team was in there and I never saw them.
  • Disable WinRM and Remote Registry early. After talking with Red team during the debrief these were some easy wins that I just seemed to have missed.
  • Stick to the script discipline all the way through. I built the rollback system for a reason. Use it the whole time!
  • Focus heavily on getting into the mail server on Day 1. The competition holders strongly emphasized how important email communication is in a work environment. I have seen it in my own IT job as well! Don't leave communication infrastructure unchecked for an entire day. It's essential for a company to run!
  • Focus on the Business side of IT too! During the audit we overstated our compliance posture because we didn't focus on it enought. Security isn't just about not letting Red team in, it's also making sure you follow the law and company policy.

Final Thoughts

Three years of qualifiers. One regional. SO WORTH IT!!!

CCDC has given me something no class can - the experience of working in a live environment under pressure, one a real team, with services that actually break and attackers that actually adapt. I made mistakes. Red team found their way into my DC. We let the auditor wait outside. We almost joined the union against the company.

But we also rotated credentials fast enough to kick red team out. We caught a social engineering attempt by reading the fine print on a fake legal document. We kept our door controlled for the entire competition.

I don't believe that I will be able to compete again next year due to looking for fulltime work, but if I had...

I would disable WinRM and Remote Registry as on of the first things.
