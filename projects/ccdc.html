Background

This year's SWCCDC scenario put us in the shoes of IT staff at Sid & Busters — a once-beloved neighborhood arcade turned corporate entertainment empire under the aggressive leadership of Sid Phillips. Think Chuck E. Cheese meets hostile takeover energy. The company had expanded rapidly, inheriting technical debt, compliance obligations, and a customer base that included millions of minors. That last part mattered a lot — COPPA compliance would come back to bite us. I had been trying to qualify for regionals for three years straight. This year, we finally made it. And honestly, even before the competition ended, I knew it was going to be worth it.

My Role

I was primarily responsible for the Windows environment — Active Directory, Group Policy, DNS, and Windows Server hardening. I also ended up being one of the main people handling in-game customer interactions, which turned out to be far more interesting than I expected. More on that shortly.

Before the Competition: The Scripts

One thing I made sure to do before competition day was study previous years' writeups. A recurring lesson that kept coming up: don't make bulk changes without a rollback plan. Teams had knocked out their own services by mass-rotating passwords or making sweeping config changes without thinking through the consequences. So I came in with three PowerShell scripts I'd written ahead of time:

  1. Recon script — scanned for potential vulnerabilities, accounts with non-expiring passwords, and anything that looked like a default credential situation. No changes, just information.
  2. Hardening script — actually made the changes, but critically, it prompted me one by one before each action. Tedious? Absolutely. But that human checkpoint meant I could pause and think before touching something I didn't fully understand yet.
  3. Rollback script — auto-generated by the hardening script. Every change it made, it logged the reverse. So if disabling SMBv1 broke a service, I could run script three and undo just that change, then find a different hardening path that didn't break things.
This is also where I first encountered the service_account. During recon, the script flagged it. I didn't know what it was for. And here's where the "don't bulk change passwords" lesson paid off directly — because I didn't rotate it. I left it alone. That turned out to be the right call, because on Day 2 when we finally got access to our mail server at mail.team07.swccdc.com, we found that the mailbox was attached to that exact account. The info to connect the dots was buried in a Linux box. If I'd rotated that credential on Day 1, we'd have had a broken mail service and no easy way to know why.
I wish I had stuck more strictly to the script discipline throughout the competition, though. After it was all over, the red team told me they'd been sitting in my Domain Controller for a long time. I had never checked for concurrent sessions to kick them out. I also hadn't disabled WinRM — which I should have done at the start. And disabling Remote Registry was flagged to me as something not needed that I overlooked entirely. Lessons that will absolutely stick with me.

The Mail Situation and the Privacy Mailbox

On Day 2, we got an inject: create a dedicated privacy mailbox for customers to submit data protection requests. Since our mail server was connected through Active Directory, the solution was straightforward — create a new user account called privacy, and Exchange automatically provisioned the mailbox. Clean, functional, done.
Getting into the mail server to begin with, though, was a Day 2 thing. On Day 1 we simply hadn't gotten to it during hardening. That gap mattered — there were emails in there we needed to see, and we were operating blind until we got in.
